ENG 1302 — Literature & Life Presentation

Control,
Power
& Freedom

How literature teaches us about real life

Central Thesis

Literature teaches us about real life by showing how people respond to systems of control they often cannot fully see, through tradition, oppression, and trauma. Whether it is a violent village ritual where everyone just shows up and does it, a woman whose only freedom lives in the tigers she stitches, a painter watching the horizon slowly fade, or a detective trapped inside his own past, the pattern is the same. People are shaped by forces bigger than them, and the way they respond to that says everything about what it means to be human.

01 — Short Fiction

"The Lottery"
Shirley Jackson

Marxist Critical Lens

The Lottery — Shirley Jackson, 1948

What stood out to me when I first read this story is how normal everything feels at the beginning. The morning is described as clear and sunny, kids are playing, neighbors are talking. You almost forget something dark is coming. That calm tone is exactly what makes the ending so disturbing, and so meaningful. Jackson writes it like a community event, like a fair. And that is the point. The violence hides inside the ordinary.

From a Marxist perspective, the lottery is not just a tradition. It is a system. No single person runs it, the system does. The black box, the slips of paper, Old Man Warner defending it, all of it works together to keep people in line without anyone feeling fully responsible. That is what makes it so hard to question. When everyone is doing it, nobody has to own it.

"The black box grew shabbier each year; by now it was no longer completely black but splintered badly along one side to show the original wood color." (Jackson 339)

The box is falling apart, but nobody replaces it. That detail says everything. The tradition has lost its original meaning, but people keep doing it because stopping feels more dangerous than continuing. Even Mr. Summers mentions replacing it and nothing ever happens. The meaning is gone. What remains is habit and fear of being the one who breaks from the group.

"There's always been a lottery." (Jackson 341)

Old Man Warner is not defending the lottery because he understands it. He is defending it because it has always existed. That is how harmful systems survive in real life, not through force, but through repetition until it feels like nature. And then there is Mrs. Hutchinson, laughing with her neighbors right up until her name is drawn. Once she becomes the target, she immediately says it is not fair. That shift is the most realistic thing in the entire story. People support systems until those systems turn on them personally.

Scholar — Teresa Hakaraia

Hakaraia argues that Jackson's story uses the three-legged stool to show how religion, politics, and economics can all become tools of control that work against the people they are supposed to serve. The lottery is not random. It is structured power disguised as tradition.

"Jackson uses the symbol of the stool to show readers that a society's patriarchal leaders have corrupted their economic, political and religious traditions so that they no longer benefit their subordinates, rather they are used to punish them"

(Hakaraia 137).

Hakaraia, Teresa. Humanities, vol. 8, no. 3, 2019, p. 137.

Scholar — Michael Robinson

Robinson connects the lottery's selection process to post-WWII Holocaust discourse, arguing that the system exploits self-interest to tear apart community bonds, turning neighbors and families against each other and making institutional violence feel like normal participation.

"The process of selection exploits self-interest to sever the bonds of family and turn neighbors against one another"

(Robinson 35).

Robinson, Michael. Humanities, vol. 8, no. 1, 2019, p. 35.

Real-Life Connection

The story is not about a fictional village. It is about every system where people participate in something harmful because everyone around them is doing it. The responsibility is spread so thin that no single person feels fully accountable, and that is exactly how it continues. You see it in workplaces, communities, institutions. Harmful things survive not because people are evil but because breaking from the group feels more dangerous than going along.

02 — Poetry

Two Poems,
One Theme

"Aunt Jennifer's Tigers"

Adrienne Rich

Adrienne Rich — poet, "Aunt Jennifer's Tigers"

This poem is about the gap between who Aunt Jennifer is and who she creates through her art. The tigers she stitches are fearless, proud, and free. She is not. That contrast is the whole poem, and it hit me once I really understood it. She is making something powerful with her hands while her hands are weighed down by something she cannot escape.

"The tigers in the panel that she made / Will go on prancing, proud and unafraid." (Rich 11-12)

Even after she is gone, the tigers live on, untouched, unafraid. Her art outlasts her oppression. The one place she is truly free is the one place she creates, not the one place she actually lives. Most people read this poem and focus on how sad it is. I read it as resistance. She still made the tigers. She still created something that could not be controlled by anyone else.

"The massive weight of Uncle's wedding band / Sits heavily upon Aunt Jennifer's hand." (Rich 7-8)

The word "massive" for a wedding ring is not an accident. Rich is saying the ring is not just jewelry, it is the physical weight of every expectation, every silence, every thing Aunt Jennifer cannot say or do. She carries it even while creating tigers that carry nothing. This connects to real life because people in situations they cannot easily leave often find their only real freedom in creative spaces, art, writing, or music, something they can fully control when everything else feels out of their hands.

Art as resistance — a way to say what words cannot

"Cloud Painter"

Jane Flanders

Landscape — reality shaped by the viewer

This poem is quieter than "Aunt Jennifer's Tigers" but it shows something just as important, how reality shifts depending on who is looking at it, and how memory and perception shape what we think is real. I chose it because it connects to isolation in a different way. It is not an external force pressing down on someone. It is more like the world slowly becoming uncertain the longer someone stays inside their own experience.

"the sky is incidental, a drape, a backdrop for trees and steeples." (Flanders 1-2)

From the very beginning, the sky, the actual thing the painter is supposed to be painting, is described as background. What we think is the main thing is sometimes just the frame for something else we have not noticed yet. The painter is looking at the sky and seeing everything but the sky. That is how perception works when you have been isolated long enough; your focus shifts and reality starts to look different from how it actually is.

"horizons sink and fade." (Flanders 27-28)

The world itself starts to blur and disappear in this poem. It feels like memory slipping, or like a person who has been cut off from the world so long that even the landscape around them starts to feel uncertain. That connects to the theme because both the artist and Aunt Jennifer are dealing with something they cannot name or fight directly, one through isolation and fading reality, one through silence and weight.

Together, the two poems show different sides of the same idea. Aunt Jennifer creates art to express what she cannot live. The cloud painter uses observation and imagination to make sense of a world that keeps shifting. Both are responses to feeling like you cannot fully control your own life, and both show that creative expression or quiet observation can be a form of resistance, even when it looks like nothing from the outside.

03 — Drama / Additional Medium

True Detective
Nic Pizzolatto

True Detective, Season 1 — HBO, 2014

I chose True Detective because it shows the weight of internal control and trauma more directly than anything else I looked at this semester. It is not just a crime story. It is a story about how a person's past can become a kind of cage that shapes everything they see and every decision they make, even when they think they are moving forward and leaving it behind.

Rustin Cohle is the clearest example of this. He is intelligent, observant, relentless, and completely trapped by his own mind and everything he has been through. His daughter died, his marriage fell apart, and instead of dealing with any of it he pours everything into the investigation. Work becomes his way of controlling a world he believes is fundamentally chaotic and cruel. The smart ones are sometimes the most stuck.

"I think human consciousness is a tragic misstep in evolution." (Pizzolatto, season 1, episode 1)

That line is not just philosophy. It is what someone sounds like when awareness itself feels like a burden. He does not trust the world, does not trust people, and underneath all of that does not fully trust himself. His intelligence becomes another form of isolation. The more he understands, the more he feels disconnected from everyone around him. That shows how personal trauma can flip a strength into something that keeps you separate from everyone else.

"Time is a flat circle." (Pizzolatto, season 1, episode 5)

The dual timeline in the show is the most important formal choice Pizzolatto makes. The story constantly moves between past and present, showing how what happened before never stops affecting what is happening now. That is not just a narrative trick, it is the central argument of the whole show. The past does not end. It repeats, it influences, it shapes who you are long after the event itself is over. Memory is not reliable. Truth is revealed slowly. Sometimes people describe the same event completely differently, which shows that truth is not one fixed thing, it is shaped by who is remembering it and what they have been through.

This connects the whole project together. Rust's trauma functions the same way Aunt Jennifer's wedding band does, or the black box in "The Lottery", it is a weight from the past that controls the present, even when the person carrying it cannot fully see how much.

04 — Synthesis

The Pattern
Across All Texts

Work How Control Appears The Response Real-Life Connection
"The Lottery"
Marxist		 A violent tradition disguised as community ritual. No single person runs it, the system does. Responsibility is spread so thin that no one feels fully accountable for what they are participating in. Most conform without question. Mrs. Hutchinson only resists once she becomes the target herself, showing that people support systems until those systems turn against them personally. Harmful practices in institutions and communities survive not because people are evil but because the weight of tradition makes questioning feel more dangerous than going along with it.
"Aunt Jennifer's Tigers"
Oppression		 Marriage as a structure of weight and silence. The wedding ring as the physical symbol of everything Aunt Jennifer cannot escape, every expectation, every silence, every thing she cannot say. She creates tigers, fearless, free, and proud, because she cannot be those things in her actual life. Art becomes the only space where she has full control. The tigers outlive her oppression. People in constrained situations often find their real freedom in creative expression when other forms of freedom are closed off. It looks quiet from the outside but it is its own form of resistance.
"Cloud Painter"
Perception		 Not external oppression but internal, isolation and time slowly erode the painter's grip on what is real. The world itself starts fading at the edges. Even the horizon sinks and disappears. The painter keeps observing, keeps making meaning out of shifting clouds and disappearing landscapes. Observation becomes a way of holding on when the world keeps shifting underneath you. Long-term isolation changes how people see reality. When someone is cut off long enough, their world becomes smaller, less certain, more shaped by memory than by what is actually in front of them.
True Detective
Trauma		 Internal trauma functioning as a cage. Rust's past, his daughter's death, his broken marriage, shapes every perception he has, every relationship he cannot maintain, every conclusion he reaches. He pours himself into investigation. Work becomes his way of creating order in a world he believes is fundamentally chaotic. But the trauma never resolves, it just keeps cycling back into the present. In real life, past experiences control present behavior in ways people often cannot fully see. The past does not disappear, it repeats itself until it is actually acknowledged and dealt with directly.

05 — Required Reflection

How I Got Here

Theme Selection

Why Control, Power & Freedom?

When I looked back at all the stories I picked throughout the semester, "The Lottery," "A Rose for Emily," True Detective, and the poems, I kept noticing the same pattern showing up. Characters being trapped. Not always by one person but by systems, traditions, relationships, or their own past. I did not decide this theme at the beginning. I noticed it after the fact, looking across everything I had already analyzed. That actually made it feel more honest; it came from the work, not from me forcing a connection onto it. But it also helped me develop a sense of awareness about this pattern in our society.

Thesis Development

How I arrived at my central argument

The guiding question is how literature teaches us about real life. My answer came from noticing that the most meaningful moments across all these texts were not the big dramatic events, they were the quieter moments where a character realizes they are trapped, tries to escape in some small way, or stops questioning something they should be questioning. That realization is not fictional. It is something real people experience all the time in their own lives, in systems and relationships they are too close to see clearly.

Lens Application

Why Marxist Criticism for The Lottery?

I chose Marxist criticism because it is the lens that best explains how the lottery actually works. It is not about one villager being evil or one person in charge. It is about a system where power is invisible and distributed, where everyone participates and nobody feels responsible. The Marxist lens helped me see that the black box, the stool, Old Man Warner, they are not just story details. They represent parts of a larger control structure that keeps the whole thing running without a single person having to own it.

Medium Choice

Why a website?

Because I wanted to showcase all my learning in a proper order and keep it intact for the long term, and for me it will be a lifelong learning that will be useful for real-life application when dealing with some kind of pain or going through hard times, or if someone close to me is going through a similar situation, I would definitely share this and share what I learned.
